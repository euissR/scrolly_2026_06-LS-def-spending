Introduction

Defence budgets across Europe are rising. Russia’s ongoing war on Ukraine, combined with doubts over the future of US security guarantees have prompted European governments to invest more in defence.

 

In real terms, European defence spending was 45% higher in 2025 than it was in 2022. This marks an important step change.

 

One challenge is sustaining this spending. Rebuilding Europe’s military capacity will require investment over many years. Yet many national budgets are under pressure from fiscal constraints, higher borrowing costs, rising energy costs and competing domestic priorities. This creates a risk that Europe’s defence ambitions will outstrip the resources available to meet them.

 

A second challenge is spending well. Higher expenditure will translate into greater military capability only if resources are directed towards the right priorities and used more efficiently.[CD3.1] That means spending cooperatively whenever possible to reap economies of scale and reduce fragmentation. It also means spending on the right priorities, by combining high-end systems with cheap mass.

 

Europe’s defence financing instruments all have a key role to play in meeting these dual challenges. This landscape has grown increasingly more complex. National budgets remain the main pillar of defence expenditure, but they are now part of a wider ecosystem of EU and NATO instruments, public financial institutions like the European Investment Bank and increasingly also private capital.

A growing number of proposals are now on the table to further expand and solidify this ecosystem. These include a repeat of SAFE, EU defence bonds, a larger EU defence budget, [AV4.1]multilateral borrowing mechanisms outside the EU framework, a Defence, Security and Resilience Bank, a larger role for public banks like the EIB, and efforts to mobilise more private capital. These options differ in feasibility, fiscal implications and likely added value.

This piece maps the current landscape of European defence financing and then assesses how the different options compare. It shows what each financing channel can realistically contribute, where its main constraints lie, and how Europe can combine national, EU, multilateral, public-bank and private financing to sustain higher defence investment and improve the efficiency of European defence spending.

Europe’s Defence Financing Ecosystem

The Core: National Spending

 

EU Member States have been significantly increasing their defence expenditures since Russia’s annexation of Crimea in 2014.

 

In the 8 years between 2014 and 2022 the aggregated nominal increase corresponded to €62 bln, namely +38%.

 

Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 caused an even greater ramp-up in military expenditure, and in fact the increase between 2022 and 2024 matched the increase between 2014 and 2022.

 

Finally, in 2025 alone (+ € 61.25), defence spending grew almost as much as it had over the 2022-24 period (+ € 62.36). In fact, 2025 saw the “sharpest annual growth in spending in Central and Western Europe since the end of the Cold War.” Spending is set to grow further, with Member States that are also NATO allies (except Spain) pledging to increase spending to 3.5 per cent to by 2032, plus 1.5% for defence related expenditure.

 

Beyond National Budgets: Mapping Europe’s Defence-Funding Instruments

As defence expenditures have grown, so has the range of funding streams that is complementing national budgets. EU and NATO instruments, funding from public banks and private finance all play a role in different ways. Each of these funding streams can contribute in different ways to giving momentum and coherence to Europe’s defence ramp up: some provide grants, others provide loans or guarantees; some are designed to support innovation, others focus on production capacity; some are designed explicitly to foster co-operation and others are not.
