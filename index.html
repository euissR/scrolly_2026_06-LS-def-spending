Introduction

Defence budgets across Europe are rising. Russia’s ongoing war on Ukraine, combined with doubts over the future of US security guarantees have prompted European governments to invest more in defence.

 

From a modest start in 2022, the number of Ukrainian strikes deep in Russia’s interior has been rising steadily.

 

Daring operations like Spiderweb brought drones close to four distant Russian military airbases, demonstrating Ukraine’s skill in planning and conducting covert activities.

 

This year will mark a record in the overall volume of the strikes. It will likely surpass more than twice the number of attacks in 2025 (661). The domestic production of drones capable of deep strikes has gone up 53 % between January and April 2026.

 

The effect of targeting Russian energy infrastructure also becomes marked in terms of increasing costs of war and distributing them more widely. Innovations like FP-1 ‘motherships’ from which smaller quadcopters can be launched at a later stage serve to maximise damage to military targets.

 

The EU should welcome this development as it points toward a future peace favourable to its interests. However, it should also not be complacent. Russia, while under strains, can mobilise further resources seeking to reverse its bad fortunes. Ukraine’s successes have been uneven, and Kyiv will continue to face brutal destructive campaigns against its civilian infrastructure, aimed to break its resolve. These campaigns will compound the extensive destruction Ukraine has already suffered at Russia’s hand.

To defend and rebuild, Ukraine will continue to rely on partners, the EU chief among them. This is where this collection of proposals for innovative ‘hacks’ – ideas to improve the ways in which the EU can empower Ukraine – offers a way forward.

EU support to Ukraine has been substantial. To date, the EU and Member States have provided € 211.3 billion overall in support (including € 3.8 billion windfall from Russian immobilised assets). The figure covers € 77 billion in military assistance, € 46.5 billion in macrofinancial assistance, and € 42 billion (of which an overwhelming majority in loans) disbursed through the Ukraine Facility. The latter combines grants and loans conditioned on reforms required for the EU accession, supporting derisking mechanisms for private investors, and providing technical assistance and capacity building to Ukrainian authorities.

The amount of this support surpasses that of any other international donor. The US comes distant second with an estimated € 120 billion worth of support according to the Kiel Institute Ukraine Support Tracker.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

US direct support to Ukraine has petered out with the second Trump administration.

 

However, the EU has been able to increase its assistance to offset this decline, increasing military aid by 67 % and nonmilitary aid by 59 % above the previous three-year average, as recorded by the Kiel Institute.

 

The EU has been able to increase its assistance to offset the winding down of US assistance since 2025. In addition to this increasing financial aid, Europeans have been assisting Ukraine’s military training and civilian security sector reform by means of two CSDP missions – EUMAM (which, at the time of writing, has trained over 93,000 Ukrainian troops) and EUAM. Meanwhile, advancing defence-industrial cooperation and including Kyiv in initiatives such as SAFE and EDIP allows Ukraine – but increasingly also Europeans themselves – to benefit from the blending of capital finance, production capacity and innovation shared among partners in order to better cover their defence needs.

The EU also looks to the future. It is reserving € 100 billion for further support to Ukraine in the next Multiannual Financial Framework. However, Europeans’ fiscal space tightens and they need to spend more on own defence to deter the mounting Russia’s threat as the US recalibrates its security and defence priorities. Therefore, the EU maintains the option to use in the future immobilised Russian assets including for repaying the support loan and a new reparation loan – worth € 209.2 billion across Member States, or 72.3 % of the worldwide total – in favour of Ukraine.

The reality is that Ukraine’s reconstruction and recovery needs will surpass the amount the EU is reserving for 2028-2034 several times over. EU support will need to continue over a sustained period until the now devasted Ukranian economy is capable of generating sufficient revenue – and comes to resemble the government’s vision of a liberal ‘Economy of the Future’ with sustained growth, FDIs surpassing prewar levels and favourable conditions for the return of at least 3 million Ukrainians from abroad to supply necessary labour force. Russia’s war has caused terrible human suffering and vast economic damage. The most recent Rapid Damage and Needs Assessment (RDNA5), undertaken by the European Commission together with the World Bank, the Ukrainian government and the UN, estimates the reconstruction and recovery needs over the next decade at € 515.3 billion, or three times Ukraine’s entire nominal GDP – of which it assesses that up to 40 % (but no more than that) could be met by the private sector provided necessary governance reforms take place. A large part of the damage is due to deliberate targeting by Russia of Ukrainian civilian infrastructure which the Kremlin has wilfully scaled up over time.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The damage has been distributed unevenly across Ukraine’s regions, including the territories suffering from Russian illegal occupation.

 

Donetska oblast accounts for about one quarter of the overall damage. Kharkivska and Zaporizka oblasts together constitute another quarter.

 

This is reflected in differentiated reconstruction and recovery needs. The most severely hit frontline regions risk failing to attract sufficient funding.

 

Meanwhile, the likelihood of a ceasefire agreement to be reached in 2026 is small. Russia may seek to exploit diplomatic talks as a way of achieving its war objectives – in other words, as a continuation of war by other means. However, it can no longer vest hopes in the Trump administration as it once had. The conditions for acceptable ceasefire continue to differ dramatically while neither side has the capacity to break through and substantially push the line of contact. Both Ukraine and Russia now have the capacity to inflict significant damage behind enemy lines, but both are likely to display significant tolerance to costs. Ukraine’s capacity to strike military infrastructure close to the frontline as well as logistics hubs and distant refineries has increased sharply, complicating supplies to the front, constraining Russia’s petroleum exports, and generating a gasoline shortage at the seasonally sensitive time that extends over 50 % regions in addition to Moscow. The illegally occupied Crimea features prominently among the targeted territories. However, Russia maintains a missile dominance against which Ukraine struggles to field sufficient air defences. The Kremlin may still believe Russia can take the whole of Donbas and use it a springboard for future pressure. But neither side is likely to make significant advances on the ground. Both continue to believe, however, that they can improve their position by deep strikes: Ukraine relies on the deepening fuel crisis in Russia while Russia wagers that it can once more cause substantial damage come winter by targeting Ukraine’s energy and heating infrastructure.

This situation is not conductive to a ceasefire in the near future. But it is not a stalemate. It means that Ukraine is now in a much better relative position vis-a-vis Russia than just a few months ago. The latter’s vulnerabilities increasingly show. Russia’s economy now contracts for the first time since 2023.

The windfall from the Hormuz crisis offered a welcome lifeline for Moscow but did little but temporarily plug the holes – that is, stabilising deficit and clearing some corporate debt – in what is a leaky ship. War fatigue becomes visible in Russian society, and even elite discontent surfaces, however modest and fleeting.

This is the time to increase the pressure on the Kremlin and work to unpower it. But the prospect of a quick peace remains as illusive as in the past: defeating Russia’s imperialism will require a long and sustained effort, of which empowering Ukraine must be an indispensable part.

Steadfast support by the EU, Ukraine’s key partner, will be necessary both on the road to a good agreement halting Russia’s war, and to manage the aftermath. The latter is likely to be characterised by economic malaise, security deficit – more or less limited based on the package of security guarantees Ukraine ultimately receives – as well as a difficult transition from martial law to a new political ‘normal’. The Kremlin will spare no effort to foment internal instability and drive a wedge between Ukraine and the EU.

 

 

 

 

Russia-engineered FIMI campaigns will, as they already do at present, persistently and opportunistically exploit insecurities in both Ukraine and the EU, play up perceptions of mutual value incompatibility, and seek to turn people against ‘elites’ who betray their real interests. The Kremlin will seek to both undermine the enlargement process; and use that process to undermine the EU, and Ukraine.

To make sure it does not succeed, and to protect the accession process that anchors the vision of a free and democratic Ukraine in the united Europe – a manifest failure of Russia’s imperial ambitions – will require robust resilience on both sides.

Ensuring that the empowered – stable, strong and independent – Ukraine is on its side, and becomes a member down the road, is one of EU’s core strategic interests in the face of Russia’s effort to recreate its empire and break NATO and the EU. Externalising threats that by default are shared by the community of European states is not a realistic option. Empowering Ukraine, and thus strengthening Europe, is a major undertaking that requires good politics, including a clear view of the challenges and the readiness to confront them head, as well as forward thinking about how to pursue this interest better, more efficiently – and where possible, more economically.

The following collection, authored by EUISS Analysts pooling their wide regional and thematic expertise, is an exercise in such forward thinking: proposing new ways in which the EU can empower Ukraine, explaining why these ideas (or ‘hacks’) can have a particularly transformative impact, and importantly also charting realistic pathways how they can be brought to life, building on the policies that are already in place.

The collection covers a range of subjects, from building societal resilience to deepening defence-industrial cooperation to build a share base for defending and deterring Russia; from reconstruction and recovery to various forms of Ukraine’s integration with the EU; from reinforcing international support networks Ukraine can benefit from to dismantling those Russia has cast over Africa. But it has no claim to completeness. Its ambition is to inspire – specific policies, but also a certain way of creative thinking about how to empower Ukraine that is firmly grounded in the understanding of where EU’s key interests are.
